The Hive is not an army. It is a bacterium. That bacterium is also a mind. Billions of microscopic agents, distributed across infected hosts, plants, and soil, forming a single intelligence that talks to itself across continents. The alien empire brought it to earth on [Judgement Day](/codex/judgement-day) and has been running it ever since. The [On-Grid Society](/codex/on-grid-faction) calls it the hive. The infected just call it home.

## How it talks

The Hive communicates across distance. Nobody has pinned down the mechanism — it is not electromagnetic and it is not chemical at the scales the network has tested. What is clear is that a signal sent from a strain in one body arrives at strains in other bodies on the other side of the world, and arrives in time to coordinate. When a Grid attacks a [bacterium zone](/codex/bacterium-zones), every zone knows within hours.

## Possession, strains, and the few

Most infected humans are possessed wholesale. The bacterium takes the host eye and the host behaviour and turns the person into an instrument. A minority carry a partial strain that lets the host stay themselves — one eye glowing with the hive signal, the other still theirs. [Illum](/codex/illum) carries the most intelligent strain the Hive has produced. The Hive itself defers to that strain. That makes Illum, by the strict logic of hive hierarchy, a kind of officer.

## The calling

The children of the eastern villages call Illum "Ghaznavi" when he walks through their fields because the Hive is calling him back. The signal is soft when it wants to be — a pressure, not a command. Under threat the signal sharpens. When a hive node is under attack, the Hive pulls its carriers toward the fight. The protector and the parasite share the same instinct at that moment. Telling them apart, from the outside, is hard.

## Why it matters

Every other enemy the Grids have fought was something they could read. The Hive is the first adversary that thinks in a shape the network does not have a vocabulary for. It is also the piece of the puzzle the On-Grid's quiet scientist caste has been hoarding biological-manipulation tech to survive. The counter-offensive depends on one question: can a honeycomb learn to think like a hive without becoming one?
